Interrupting Data transmission

We have a big problem. The sun is between Mars and Earth. Our data encounters a
major barrier. In this case, data arrives at the destination with a delay. We want to minimize this
waste of time. Which system do you think is more useful for us?

DTN
Delay/Disruption Tolerant Networking (DTN) is NASA’s solution to reliable internetworking for
space missions.
DTN provides assured delivery of data using automatic store-and-forward mechanisms.
Each data packet that is received is forwarded immediately if possible, but stored for future
transmission if forwarding is not currently possible but is expected to be possible in the future.
Advantage:
● DTN enables more reliable and efficient data transmissions resulting in more usable
bandwidth. DTN also improves link reliability by having multiple network paths and
assets for potential communication hops.
The DTN protocol suite allows for many priority levels to be set for different data types,
ensuring that the most important data is received ahead of less important data.
Disadvantages
● Data transmission time becomes longer

The communications system consists of a satellite in orbit around Mars, one
in orbit around the sun, and ground stations on Earth.
The satellite over the Mars settlement is an areostationary satellite,
the Mars equivalent of a geostationary satellite. It is always
in the same place in the sky on Mars, receiving data from the
settlement and transmitting it to Earth. On Earth the data is
received by ground stations using large satellite dishes. The areostationary
satellite enables almost 24/7 communication, which is interrupted
only when Mars is in between the satellite and the Earth.
This is solved by placing a second satellite in an orbit
around the sun. It will follow the same orbit as the Earth does but
trails 60 degrees behind it in the L5 Lagrangian point of the Sun-Earth
system. With this second satellite in place, when Mars is in between the
areostationary satellite and the Earth, the signal can be relayed by the
second satellite. Once every 26 months, the Sun is exactly
in between Mars and the Earth. This occultation lasts about six
weeks. The second communications satellite will also be used to relay
signals during this period.
Advantages:
● Elimination of this 2-week data transmission interruption.
Disadvantages:
● When the Sun is in between Mars and the Earth and at the same time
Mars is in between the areosynchronous satellite and the second
satellite, we will have no contact with Mars for about two hours.

The Corruption of Transmitted Data Between 2 Planets

There is distortion in the data sent to us from Mars due to electromagnetic waves from the sun.
This reduces the quality of the data. How can this problem be overcome?

DSOC
Communications between space and ground will employ novel,
advanced lasers in the near-infrared region of the electromagnetic
spectrum. These systems will be capable of delivering information
rates at least 10 times faster than conventional systems
that use comparable mass and power.
Key DSOC technologies developed for the project
include: a low-mass spacecraft disturbance isolation and pointing
assembly; a high-efficiency flight laser transmitter; and a pair
of high-efficiency photon counting detector arrays
for the flight optical transceiver and the ground-based receiver.
These technologies are integrated into the DSOC flight laser transmitter
and ground-based receiver to enable photon-efficient communications
with the capability to discern faint laser signals from background "noise" contributed
by solar energy scattered by the Earth's atmosphere.
Advantages:
● Reduces background noise.
